Ground shock isn't one number. It's a range.

A Gaussian process trained on simulated blast data, reporting confidence bands that are checked against reality instead of just claimed.

Eight hundred shots trained it. A hundred more, held back until the very end, tested it. Every one of its confidence bands gets checked against what actually happened, not just asserted.

A single number always lies a little.

Blast engineers compress two numbers, standoff distance and charge weight, into one: scaled distance, Z = R ÷ W1/3. Plot peak particle velocity against Z on a log-log grid and a straight line falls out — the UFC power law, V = K·Z−n. It's a good line.

It's also a lie by omission. Two shots at the same scaled distance can differ by a factor of two, because no two patches of soil couple a shock the same way. A single predicted number quietly erases that scatter. This project keeps the power law as a starting point, then lets a Gaussian process learn how the scatter itself changes with distance — so what comes out the other end is a range, with its width earned from real data, not made up.

A thousand simulated shots.

Split 800 for training, 100 for tuning, 100 held back completely until the very end. Every point carries its own noise level — not one number borrowed for the whole dataset.

The same scatter, given somewhere to stand.

A third axis: how densely these simulated shots actually cluster in scaled-distance/velocity space. Tall ridges are where the sampling landed thick; low ground is where it's thin — worth knowing before trusting the model equally everywhere.

A kernel that knows its own uncertainty.

A Matérn-5⁄2 Gaussian process wrapped around that power-law mean, fit in log-log space. What makes it heteroscedastic is simple: every training point comes in with its own measured noise level, so the covariance matrix's diagonal isn't one shared number — it's as many numbers as there are points. Bands widen where the data is genuinely noisier, not everywhere equally.

That band, walked across every distance and every charge.

The chart above is a slice through one scaled distance at a time. Here the 90% band's own width becomes the surface — a second landscape, showing exactly where in physical (distance, charge weight) space the model is least sure of itself.

Say 90% confident. Mean it.

A 90% band is a promise: nine times out of ten, reality should land inside it. Held-out test points, never seen during fitting, check whether that promise holds at four different levels.

With only a hundred points, one level landing a few percent off its target isn't a red flag — it's sampling noise, confirmed by re-checking against a much larger held-out sample.

The same check, as a corridor.

The shaded slab is the ±5% tolerance from sampling noise. The dashed line is what perfect calibration looks like. Each stem shows exactly how far, and in which direction, a tested level actually landed — inside the corridor is fine, poking through a wall is worth a second look.
